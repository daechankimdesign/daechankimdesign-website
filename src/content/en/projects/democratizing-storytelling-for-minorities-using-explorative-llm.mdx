## About

The web app is where Oria stops being a recorder and becomes a place: a shareable, explorable home for personal stories, so the people they are for can actually reach them.

Oria collects stories on mobile, through a conversation with Oria AI. The web app is what those stories open into. It turns a private recording into something a family, and a wider community, can browse, explore by asking, and pass on. Where the app focuses on getting a story in, the web app is built around getting it out: to the right person, at the right moment.

## The Problem

Most personal stories that do get recorded still go nowhere. A voice memo, a video on one phone, a document in a drawer: even when the recording is digital, it stays locked to one device and one person, unsorted and unsearchable. The memory exists, but no one can find it when it matters.

Underneath that is a quieter inequity. The lessons and histories of ordinary people, and especially of the older and overlooked, rarely become part of any shared record. History and common knowledge are written by the few with the means to publish, and everyone else's experience stays private and is lost a generation later. Collecting a story is the easy half. The convention worth rebuilding is the one that leaves those stories unreachable.

## The Insight

People do not sit and read an archive. They arrive with a question, what was my grandmother's first apartment like, how did our family come to be here, and they want the one story that answers it. A static list of recordings cannot serve that. An archive you can ask can.

That pointed the whole web app at exploring rather than browsing. The same language technology that lets someone tell a story by talking should let someone else find it by asking, which is what makes the archive feel less like storage and more like a conversation with a family's memory.

## The Web App

The web app's job is to take a story from recorded to reachable, and to do it without the clutter that made the early product hard for its own users.

**A story becomes a place you can visit.** Each recording from Oria AI arrives as a multimedia record, the voice, the video, the photos, and the text together, presented to be read, watched, or listened to. The three principles that shaped the product, simplicity, empathy, and clarity, carry over to the larger desktop canvas, so a story is calm to sit with rather than busy to decode.

**Exploring by asking.** This is where the explorative LLM lives. Instead of scrolling a list, a visitor asks the archive a question and is taken to the story that answers it, and from there to the ones connected to it. Exploration replaces navigation: you follow a thread of memory rather than hunt through folders.

## Role

Founding product lead. I extended Oria's teller-first thinking from collection to consumption: designing how a recorded story is presented, explored, and shared on the web, and shaping the explorative experience that the language model makes possible.

## Learnings

**Access is preservation.** A story no one can reach is not really saved. Preserving it is not finished at recording; it is finished when the right person can find it.

**An archive should answer, not just store.** The strongest idea on the web side was to let people explore by asking rather than by scrolling. It is the same lesson Oria keeps teaching: find the convention that serves the system, a tidy list, instead of the person, a question they want answered, and rebuild it around the person.
