# Oria Web App Case Study Guide

This case study (`democratizing-storytelling-for-minorities-using-explorative-llm`)
should center the **web app**, not the whole product. The title is a consumption
claim: *democratizing storytelling* is about who gets to tell stories and who gets
to reach them, which is exactly what the web app does. Mobile and Oria AI are the
**collection** pipeline; the web app is where stories are **opened up, explored,
and shared**.

Use [[Case Study Format]] for section order and [[Design Ethos]] for voice. Same
non-negotiables as the other case studies: first person, candid, short
declarative headings, **no em-dashes**, no invented metrics, `<MDXImage>` with
one-line captions.

---

## 1. The thesis (decide this first)

One sentence that the whole piece defends. Draft:

> Recording a story is only half of preserving it; a story is preserved only when
> the people it is for can actually reach and explore it, and the web app is where
> Oria turns private recordings into a living, explorable record for a wider circle.

Pin down the **convention being rebuilt** (the Ethos move). For the web app it is
not "older people struggle with apps" (that was the mobile/collection problem).
It is: **personal stories, even once recorded, stay siloed and unsearchable, so
the lessons of ordinary and overlooked people never reach the wider community.**
History and shared knowledge are written by the few; the web app lets the many
contribute to and draw from it.

## 2. The "explorative LLM" angle (the differentiator)

The slug and title promise an *explorative LLM*. Make the web app the place that
delivers it. The collection side uses the LLM to **take stories in** (Oria AI
conversation); the web app should use the LLM to **let people explore stories
out**: ask a question and surface the right memory, discover connections across an
archive, follow a thread instead of scrolling a static list. Spell out, in the
Design section, exactly what "explorative" means here. (See open questions.)

## 3. Section-by-section brief

Follow the format arc. For each section: the angle to take, plus what you need to
supply.

1. **Title + summary** (already set). Title: *"Democratizing storytelling for
   minorities using explorative LLM."* Summary: keep a short web-app-leaning
   support line. Consider tightening the current one toward access/sharing, e.g.
   "An explorable, shareable home for personal stories, so a wider community can
   reach and learn from them." (Your call.)
2. **Metadata row.** `Role` (web app design lead / founding product lead) ·
   `Platform` Web · `Year` · `Team` (solo or with whom). Keep it quiet.
3. **About** (one sentence). What the web app *is*, plainly: the place where
   stories recorded in Oria become explorable and shareable beyond one device or
   one person.
4. **Outcome** (results first, 2 to 3 sentences + hero image). What the web app
   delivered. State honestly whether it shipped or is a concept; describe what it
   demonstrated. No invented numbers.
5. **The Problem** (1 to 2 paragraphs, reframed). Open with a concrete hook (a
   recorded story that no one ever revisits; a grandparent's memory trapped on one
   phone). Then reframe to the access/democratization problem above. Keep the
   collection problem to a single sentence of context.
6. **Research / Insight** (1 to 2 paragraphs). How families and communities
   actually want to *consume* and *share* stories, and the insight that pointed to
   an explorable archive rather than a static one. Quote real people if you have
   them.
7. **The Web App** (the Design / System section, go deep here). Cover, with
   images: how a multimedia story record is presented for reading, listening, and
   watching; the sharing and access model (Oria connections, family vs wider
   community, privacy); and the **explorative LLM** in action. Tie each choice
   back to the three pillars (simplicity, empathy, clarity) in a web/desktop
   context. This is the heart of the piece.
8. **Role.** One short paragraph: your responsibilities on the web app
   specifically.
9. **Learnings** (2 to 3 candid reflections). At least one web-app-specific
   reflection, e.g. "access is preservation: an archive no one can navigate is not
   preserved," and one honest gap.

## 4. What to carry over vs. trim

The existing draft covers all three surfaces. To refocus:

- **Trim to context:** the mobile redesign and the three-pillars deep dive, and
  the Oria AI conversation, become a short "how stories get in" paragraph that
  sets up the web app. Keep the teller-vs-collector idea as one line; do not
  re-run it as the main problem.
- **Expand:** everything about consumption, sharing, access, and the explorative
  LLM, which the current draft only touches in the "Opening It Up" section.
- **Reuse:** the voice, the Ethos framing, and the "redesign is discipline" and
  "easiest interface is sometimes no interface" learnings if they still fit.

## 5. Open questions (only you can answer these)

The source notes are thin on the web app, so the draft will stall without these:

- **Shipped or concept?** Is the web app live, in development, or a concept? This
  sets the Outcome's honesty.
- **What does "explorative LLM" actually do on the web?** Search, ask-the-archive
  Q&A, surfacing related memories, generating summaries or connections, guided
  reading? Be specific; this is the whole hook.
- **Who consumes, and how?** Family only, or a wider/public community? Reading,
  listening, watching, or all three? Any access or privacy controls?
- **What is the core web flow?** From "a story exists" to "the right person
  explores it." A 3 to 5 step flow would anchor the Design section.
- **What did you actually design here** (vs. the mobile/AI side)? Your specific
  role and contributions on the web app.
- **Any real signal?** Usage, feedback, a pilot, anything true you can cite. If
  none, we keep it honest and concept-framed.
- **Real assets?** Web app screens to replace the picsum placeholders, with a
  one-line intended caption each.
